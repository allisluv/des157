Alli's DES 157 Project Comparative Research

The two most important concepts I want to work with are a goal of teaching and an audience of young students. After examining the viddiverse and Khan Academy as developmental platforms aimed at students, many important issues have been brought to my attention.

First, it occurred to me that it is important to ensure that a site for kids is safe and secure. viddiverse is a website that was put online in 2014, but no longer exists, perhaps because of security concerns. When signing up for an account on the site, a kid is prompted to enter their parent’s email, providing the parents with access to surveillance and account permissions. Though the advertisement for the site makes it seem thorough, nothing prevents a twelve-year-old from entering their own email account and setting their own permissions without the parents knowledge. Khan Academy, on the other hand, does not require parental permissions. The site doesn’t allow for content from users and is not intended for socializing (aside from comments and questions that are probably monitored), so there is little risk associated with stumbling across something inappropriate or someone suspicious. This means that it would be very important to find a balance of limiting the content that users can upload to the site and finding a way for parents to monitor their children.

Second, it is very important to be able to appeal to a wide age range of kids. The general graphics on the viddiverse site made use of bright colors and animations to attract attention. It also seemed appealing because it aspired to be a social media platform reminiscent of YouTube and facebook, and so allowed younger kids to get involved with the similar sites to the ones they aspired to get involved with as teens. Thought the design of the Khan Academy website is more basic and simple, it encourages learning math by starting with a video and then continuing to an interactive quiz. Both sites have a way of inviting the user to participate instead of simply providing content.

A third concern I have is usability. I think it is important to get kids interested in learning while they are young, presenting the problem that they may not be very familiar with computers. A website for kids age 5-13 would need to be very easy to navigate. The viddiverse website aimed to simplify video making down to button clicking. Though typing is an important skill, kids who are just learning to use computers may get frustrated if they are required to use the keys to use the site. Khan Academy also requires very little typing in the lessons aimed at younger children.

Finally, another potential reason that the viddiverse site failed, I understand the risks of allowing children to create content for a webpage. Assuming the content that users upload is appropriate and educational, there is even still the concern that kids are completely inexperienced in teaching and could be incorrect, misleading, unclear, boring, etc. Khan Academy manages to teach effectively because the content is compiled by someone who has an idea of how to teach through an online media. My research on these two websites has given me some ideas, but also brought many concerns to my attention about dealing with user involvement on a website.
